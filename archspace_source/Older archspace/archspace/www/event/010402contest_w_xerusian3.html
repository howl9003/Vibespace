The Price of Honor
by Carlo Jan P. Garingarao


The Trak'na Star Cluster's neutral zone: stretching more than 15 parsecs of demilitarized space, 3 star systems with a total of 14 resource-rich planets. The vast uninhabited area lay deep within the star cluster, effectively marking the boundaries of the several councils that called Trak'na home.
As far as inter-council peace agreements were concerned, the neutral zone was strictly off-limits to ships, military and civilian alike. Not more than half a century ago, it was an area of intense conflict within Trak'na, each council fighting for complete control of the zone and its wealthy star systems. It was not clear which side was winning, the bitter conflict costing dearly in lives and resources, until everyone realized that fighting for the area was starting to cost them more than they could gain. The Jarnapah Protectorate, a Bosalian council, pushed for a cluster-wide ceasefire, which eventually led to the Trak'na Peace Initiative that effectively stopped the conflicts and encouraged inter-council trade and relations. The zone was soon abandoned, blockaded, all grudges forgiven and apparently forgotten.


The fleet of bulky ships slowly entered the demilitarized zone unnoticed, unhampered. The cloaking generators were working all too perfectly, the anti-matter engines adapted to leave no trace at the fleet's wake. After a month's travel, it was in the heart of Trak'na at last, with the entire star cluster oblivious to its presence.
Not far ahead stretched a grid of widely-spaced cylindrical objects, pulsing a faint green: the neutral zone barricade. The repulsion field generators were of Evintos design, establishing a formidable barrier against any foolhardy ships, with the heavily-armored generators safely situated behind the field.
Onward the fleet progressed, unmindful of the barrier that lay before it. The ships' shields flared a brilliant red as they came into contact with the field, but the vessels themselves advanced, lancing through the barricade like laser through flesh. Determining the field's fluctuation rate and adjusting the shield harmonics to match was mere child's play.
Now past their one and only obstacle to the neutral zone, the Targoid seed ships diverged, each proceeding to its designated planet for colonization.


The nekral, the most revered and most sacred of the Xerusian honor-duels. Despite the possible death of an opponent, it was still the most convenient manner of resolving conflict between two warriors, whatever the argument. The nekral could be fought anytime and anywhere the contenders agree upon, to refuse meant utter shame. It was fought with metal blades alone; no projectile, psionic or energy weapons were allowed. The loser admits defeat or, sometimes, fights to the death; and the victor has his viewpoint upheld by his triumph. It was so simple; yet so revered.

Teradin Valinor narrowed his eyes into slits, his right hand tightly gripping his anti-matter reactive broadsword, feet planted widely apart as he held a defensive stance against his opponent. Small rivers of sweat trickled down his neck, arms and chest. Combined with the sparring chamber's humid air and the full combat armor safely encasing him, it made concentrating on the nekral at hand an arduous task.
Before him, ten feet away, stood Teradin Hieronymus, also in full battle regalia. The Xerusian held a calm, almost negligent pose, blade pointed to the ground, a thin, smug smile plastered on his face. In a fleeting glance, his eyes darted to his sword's hilt and the minute controls imbedded in the pommel.
    "Don't even think about it," Valinor growled. "Swords powered down."
    Hieronymus's grin widened at the remark, "Why, my dear Valinor, are you implying that I cheat? Or are you just worried that I may be too good for you with our blades activated?"
    Without warning, he charged at full speed, polished armor and blade flashing, bringing the sword down in a diagonal slash on Valinor's left shoulder. But Valinor was ready, used to his opponent's surprise antics, bearing his blade up to meet the blow with a resounding clang. He quickly lunged forward at Hieronymus's abdomen in retaliation, which the other barely evaded by nimbly stepping sidewards.
    "Too predictable," Valinor simply announced, resuming his stance.
    The simple comment was enough to ignite Hieronymus's volatile disposition, and he charged again, this time driving his blade down on his adversary's head with all his strength. Valinor easily countered the strong blow, their swords crossed, but Hieronymus pressed downward in a fit of rage, eyes blazing. Valinor thinned his lips into a grim line, maintaining the block. Desperate to preserve his wounded ego, Hieronymus's thumb slowly inched towards the power controls. The other spotted the subtle motion, and heaved forward with all of his strength, throwing the angered Xerusian flat on his back.
    "Damn you, Hieronymus!" Valinor cursed, stepping on his opponent's blade and training his blade's tip on his exposed throat.
    The fallen Teradin held a hand up in surrender, "Good show, Valinor. I admit defeat."      
    Ignoring the blade, he picked himself up, retrieved his weapon from under Valinor, and returned it to its sheath.
    "Don't think I'm going to let that pass by," he followed suit. "You were going to power up your blade."
    "Why, my dear comrade, what ever made you utter such an accusation?"
    Before he could retort, the sparring chamber's heavy doors slid open with an audible hiss. A Xerusian dragoon entered, bowed deeply at them both, and promptly announced, "Pardon my intrusion, my lords. The Regent seeks both your audience at once, he awaits you in his private chambers."

    "Of course, the reported field fluctuations in the barricade may mean nothing at all," Percinal, Regent of the Dylanus Dominion, concluded. The large, heavyset Xerusian leaned against his cathedra, his hands steepled beneath his broad chin, flowing crimson robes of office draped over the throne's armrests.
    The throne was set upon a slightly-raised landing, while Valinor and Hieronymus knelt on the main floor of the throne room. The recent argument between the two Teradins was forgotten for the moment, the situation at hand requiring utmost attention.
    Not more than two days ago, the Jarnapah Protectorate reported a slight, yet brief, disturbance in the neutral zone barricade, and promptly asked all of the star cluster's councils for any connection with the aberration. As expected, no one stepped forward.
    "But the Evintos are not famous for manufacturing erroneous equipment, your majesty," Hieronymus reminded him. "What is their explanation for the temporary phenomenon?"
     "That is what worries me," Percinal's frown deepened. "Those metallic simpletons cannot give me a straight answer. There were no reported ships near the area, no engine exhaust traces, no large celestial objects or phenomena that may have affected the generators... nothing. Frankly, I do not trust those lifeless lackeys as much as the others do.
     "That is why I am sending the two of you and your men in an unofficial investigation to the neutral zone. Your ship is being readied as we speak, your men already notified."
     Valinor spoke for the first time since they came into the throne room. "But that is a direct violation of the peace initiative, sire. The neutral zone is..."
     "That is why this mission is unofficial, Teradin," the regent snapped. "No one needs to know. You two just get your men in the area, do a standard sweep, and leave; it is that simple. Hieronymus, I place you in command of this mission. You leave within the cycle."

     Valinor arrived at the royal spacedock half a cycle later, where a large Xerusian armed transport was already prepped for the mission. His men, twenty of the Dylanus Dominion's best-trained Dragoons, were already marching into the ship via a connecting catwalk; all except for one who waited for him: Adwar, his second-in-command and close friend.
     Adwar promptly rendered a quick bow as he drew near, which Valinor replied with a weary smile.  Despite how close they were, Adwar never forgot showing the respect their respective positions demanded.
    "A bad day, I presume?" Adwar asked as they crossed the catwalk together.
    "Let me recall: Hieronymus challenged me to a senseless nekral over preference in women, in which he clearly tried to cheat; the regent sends us to an unofficial mission which violates the Trak'na peace treaty; and we have a selfless, egocentric Teradin in charge... would you call that a bad day?" Valinor replied in an irked tone.
    "Pardon me for asking, sir."
    The access hatch slid shut behind them as they stepped into the large airlock, which was linked to an elevator by a short corridor. They proceeded up, past engineering and the dragoons' quarters, and stepped off at the command deck. A long, narrow viaduct led to the transport's bridge, spanning across a 50-foot drop over the ship's enormous engines below.
    "You're late, Valinor," Hieronymus snapped as the two entered the bridge, his back facing them. The Teradin was surveying the pilots as they readied the ship for launch, hands clasped behind his back. "Strap yourselves in at once, we've wasted enough time."
    Valinor opened his mouth to respond with a barrage of curses, but stopped himself in time and held his temper in check. Talking to a superior in a hostile manner was a grave insult, whatever the circumstances... a dictum Hieronymus knew all too well. He had no choice but to play along: it was the honorable thing to do.
    The transport's twin engines roared, propelling the craft upward past intraplanetary traffic until it disappeared into the Dominion homeworld's thick crimson clouds.
    The craft was never seen or heard of again.

    "Two icros to atmospheric entry, sir," Adwar turned from the dropship's controls and announced. "Long-range sensors indicate a wide ion field on the planet surface, layering above a large valley."
    "Any suitable landing sites outside the field?" Valinor peered at the sensor display.
    "None outside the valley. The surrounding terrain is too mountainous."
    Hieronymus's aggravated voice filtered through the communications console, "Then you'll just have to settle for the valley, Valinor."
    "At once," Valinor replied between gritted teeth, then signaled to Adwar to cut off the link. "Find a spot as near the edge of the field as possible."
Here they were, deep within the neutral zone, about to land on a restricted planet. Phase shifting the transport past the barricade was one thing, but touching down was another. Hieronymus had sent them to investigate an ion field on one of the outer planets which was impenetrable their sensors, while the transport remained at a safe distance on high orbit. The commanding Teradin might as well have labeled them as cannon fodder.
    On the main viewport, the unnamed planet's bleak, rugged landscape was etched with gaping fissures, remnants of volcanic activity that had long died out. The only break in the gray terrain was an extensive valley ringed by lofty, serrated peaks.
    "We're entering the valley now..."
    Without warning, the dropship's engines throbbed, the displays dimmed, and the ship buckled slightly. Valinor kept his balance as the ship shifted, and barked, "Report!"
    Adwar futilely scanned the flickering displays before him, "I do not know, sir. All sensor systems and shields are off-line, engines at 70%... it must be the field."
    Before Valinor could issue a command, the ship's bucking stopped, and the consoles returned on-line. But what caught their attention was the grim sight their viewport offered: massive, segmented stacks rising along the valley slopes, pointing upward and terminating in shiny spheres pulsating with energy. The stacks were arranged like the spokes of a wheel, converging in the middle of the valley floor into a gigantic metallic half-constructed dome.
    "By the regent's sword..." Adwar gasped, stunned. "Those must be emitting the ion field; sensor dampers of a sort. And that central structure..." He quickly checked his sensor console. But by then, Valinor knew exactly where they were.
     "We're above the genesis of a Targoid colony! Adwar, get us out of here!"
     The dropship slammed violently to port, hurling Valinor to the bulkhead to his left. Two sleek, small but well-armed ships had appeared out of the valley's shadows, and started to rain their craft with plasma fire.
     "Our shields are barely holding, sir!" Adwar warned, "If we pass through the field above, we will temporarily lose our shields again."
     "And be completely vulnerable to their weapons," the Teradin grimly concluded. "Evasive maneuvers... notify the rest of my men in the rear. Can we contact the main transport?"
     Adwar shook his head, "Transmissions cannot penetrate the field."
     Despite Adwar's attempts to dodge enemy fire, their dropship was slower than the Targoid scouts, and much bulkier. A shot completely vaporized the vessel's starboard engine.
     "We're going down, sir!" The gray basin floor filled the viewport at a frightening rate. Valinor scrambled to secure himself in his seat, magnetic locks fastening to his armor.

     "Still no contact with Teradin Valinor's dropship, my lord," the transport pilot reported for the third time. "It has been twenty icros since they entered the ion field."
     Hieronymus slammed his armored fist on the command chair's armrest, and cursed, "What is taking that hortuc so long?"
     "Wait..." the pilot frowned at his console, surprised. "An escape capsule from the dropship, sire, bearing Teradin Valinor."
     He rose, bewildered, then spun to leave the bridge. "Open the docking bay."

     The airlock cycled, then hissed as the double doors opened. Valinor, polished battle armor dented in at least half a dozen places, lips set in a grim line, stepped onto the docking bay.
     "What happened down there?" Hieronymus, blocking the elevator, arms crossed, demanded. "Where are your men?"
     "Shobar damn you!" Valinor spat, charging past him and stepping onto the elevator. "It's a Targoid colony down there! Only one escape capsule was functional after we crashed down, my men are still down there holding their ground against their warrior drones. We have to send another dropship down there to rescue them!"
     Hieronymus managed to enter the elevator as it started to rise to the command deck. "Watch your tone, Valinor. I am still in command of this mission, and we cannot afford a futile rescue attempt!"
     "Futile?"
     "We cannot risk staying here any longer. Those pests will send out a search swarm within icros; we are leaving."
     The elevator stopped at the command deck, and Hieronymus started across the viaduct leading to the bridge when Valinor firmly held his shoulder and whirled him around. "Why don't you tell that to my men!"
     Hieronymus knocked his arm aside and unsheathed his blade. "Are you disobeying my orders, hortuc? Challenging me to another nekral?"
     "If that's what it takes to save my men, yes!" the other followed suit and brought his eternium buckler to bear. The Teradin was clearly too arrogant to change his decision, and each second that passed by meant danger to Adwar and his dragoons. Valinor took a step forward, and was met by Hieronymus's powered-up blade.
     Valinor raised his shield, the anti-matter crackling loudly against metal. He feinted a blow to the shoulder, but quickly ducked and gouged a deep wound into Hieronymus's left leg past his battle armor. The commander cried out in pain, and collapsed on the catwalk. Valinor struck his right hand with his shield and knocked the weapon from his grip, where it plunged down the chasm and crackled on the engineering deck below.
     "Stop... you win," Hieronymus panted, pressing his hand on the bleeding wound.
     "We will send the second dropship immediately after my men."
     The subjugated Teradin nodded mutely. Valinor lowered his guard, and approached Hieronymus... and a brilliant flash erupted from his torso, followed an instant later by searing pain. He glanced down, at a gaping, smoldering hole in the middle of his chest, piercing through his mithril combat armor.     
     Dumbfounded, he raised his head at Hieronymus. The Teradin's features were contorted by a painful grin, the sword in his right hand replaced by a diminutive but lethal plasma pistol.
     "You may have won the nekral, Valinor, but you are dead. Where does that leave you?" he struggled to rise with his unwounded leg. "I'm not going to let your detestable sense of honor kill me, you know."
     "But..." was all the dying Valinor could utter, a word of utter disbelief at what Hieronymus had just violated. Breaking the rules of the nekral was punished by death, no matter how petty the issue being argued. But there was a bitter truth to Hieronymus's twisted logic: where did his honor leave him now?
Valinor felt his legs collapse under him, and he dimly realized that he had just toppled over the low catwalk railing. The moribund Teradin's last sensation was the sickening perception of free-fall...

     Less than an icro later, the Xerusian transport's engines flashed to life, and the craft accelerated to full speed towards the neutral zone barricade. Mere seconds from reaching the barrier and freedom, three colossal Targoid battlecruisers emerged out of nowhere in the transport's path, forcing it to a dead halt.
     Weapons flashed, all focused on the dwarfed craft, engulfing it in a brilliant conflagration and reducing it into a shower of spiraling debris.
