A Matter of Faith
By Patrick Ramsahoye

 Admiral Osiris Selket waited impatiently outside the Chapel. Everything about the Chapel stressed its importance. It was in the heart of the Governor's Palace. The halls leading to the Chapel had ceilings of a meager height near the outer walls of the palace. As visitors moved closer to the Chapel, the ceilings rose until they reached their apex of about fifty feet. Each doorway to the Chapel rose to almost the height of the ceiling. The doors all depicted images of The Great One amidst his avatars and celestials. This had the effect of causing any visitor to the Chapel to feel just how diminutive they truly were in the presence of the god.

 The Theocrat had just returned from the Imperial Throneworld. The Emperor rewarded the Theocrat's dutiful service with the title of Baron. As was tradition, the Theocrat's first stop on returning was at the Chapel. Selket and the other members of the Theocrat's retinue dutifully waited outside. He was not looking forward to giving his report. Too many things changed while the Theocrat was away.

  The door to the Chapel cracked open. Selket closed his eyes with a feeling of rapture as the psychic energies washed over him. His concerns faded away. The news was disturbing, but he felt a renewed sense of courage. The Theocrat stepped from the Chapel into the hall. His psi gems burned brightly on his carapace. As he walked toward his chambers the retinue fell into place and followed.

  I am puzzled, Admiral. Selket could feel the Theocrat's emotions and words push into his mind. When I left, the people were happy in their prosperity. The joy I felt from them for the honour the Emperor bestowed on me helped bolster me while I was surrounded by so many of the Others. Yet now that I have returned I sense impatience from the people on the street. The citizens shun the merchant areas set aside for our allies. Was I gone so long that so much would have changed?

  Selket formed his thoughts carefully in the deepest levels of his mind before bringing them to the surface. Our relationships with the Others are very unusual. The people willingly accepted the benefits of those alliances and treaties. Despite this, they also felt an emptiness. They wondered how all this prosperity fit to the will of the Great One. Our fleets only venture forth, it seems, to exercise or to circle the planets of the infidels like annoying insects. The Holy Warriors prepare and train for a Jihad that you seem reluctant to join. The Others are allowed to walk the streets without holding to our beliefs.

  For a brief moment the Theocrat paused in his gait. Admiral, do you remember the circumstances that made you my most senior officer? I have no desire to repeat past mistakes.

  Selket remembered the early days very well. The Theocrat pursued the Jihad with a passion. He struck at any target that was in reach. His fleets seemed unstoppable. Then came the retaliations. Conquests were wrenched away. The fleets kept for defence where annihilated against overwhelming odds. The Theocrat humbled himself and approached the Speaker of their current council for admittance. No one knows why she accepted. The Others rarely see Xeloss fanaticism as an advantage. Despite the strength of the council, the Theocrat's enemies dared a single last assault. The only ships to survive were the few out on deep space missions. Selket, an officer for not more than a week, was suddenly thrust into the role of Admiral of the Fleets.
  I remember Your Holiness. I sat with you at negotiations with the council. You trusted me with rebuilding the fleets. I led our untested ships side by side with our allies. You let me participate in your plan. Your goals are apparent to me. But I am not an ordinary citizen. What I know and see is not as apparent to them. In the end, what is true is less important than what they believe to be true. The beginning of their current discontent has long been there. It just needed a catalyst.


  And what is this catalyst?


  A demagogue, a novitiate named Apophis Maat who claims he was visited by an avatar of the Great One. Supposedly this avatar told him the Great One is impatient with our idleness. The message this prophet brings appeals to the common worshipper. Everywhere he goes, the people are moved by his words.


  Have you heard him?


  He is on the homeworld now. I met with him.


  What do you think of his claims?


  He is sincere in his beliefs. That makes his message all the more potent. For now, he only wants to meet with you, probably to challenge your methods. He does still accept you as the head of our Faith.


  If he is a messenger from the Great One, then who am I to challenge the truth of his words. Perhaps it is time we renewed our aggressive posture. Perhaps this Apophis Maat will be the perfect choice to lead our fleets to victory. We do have the new ships after all.


  Selket could not keep the surprise out of his thoughts. Surely you do not want to hand over the new fleets to someone who's only ability seems to be appealing to the desires of a mob?


  Hardly that, my friend. Your position is quite secure. The new fleets are hardly common knowledge. The older ships are outdated and are costing more to maintain than they are worth. Perhaps their best use is as a test of Maat's veracity. If he dies, then there is a new martyr to the cause.


  And if he lives?


  Then I have a new admiral who will be quite happy to spend his time bombarding the planets of our enemies rather than rousing the populations of our own worlds. Yes, I like this plan. Summon Apophis Maat to me.


  Yes, Your Holiness. Admiral Selket stopped and bowed as the Theocrat and his retinue continued.
