Prasad's Shame
by Steven Lourie


...Upon that day of Nirvana Chakravati's Ascension, he was borne on wings of thought and truth to the topmost tower of the Golden Dream Temple, his mind so freed from earthly thought the world could scarce hold him. It was from here that he spoke to the large gathering of his fellow acolytes and PsiWeavers, giving them words of hope;
' Hear me, Followers of The Way. Hear, for I have attained the Goal of Goals, the Light of Lights! I see no shadows, I hear no lies, and my way has been made as clear as the division of Heaven and Earth. Steady on The Blessed Way must you all travel, with no pause of regret or with the hesitation of doubt...'

'...Spurn the base and the elemental, cast aside the false and the unjust, and be as if born again into the World of The Way! I have done as an example to you all, to all the lowly and wicked that do not hear their souls weep tears of pure sorrow for their twisted live. Live not for the Image, but for the Essence!'

'...Fill your being with love, honor and life, for that is the firmament by which your soul will be guided to the Goal of Goals. Love one another and ask no more than you would of yourself¡¦nor conceive of any lie that would seem to make your life-travel easier...'

When at last Nirvana Chakravati's time had passed and his words ceased flowing from his mind of flawless wisdom, he spread out his arms, his hands burning with golden light that made a shadow of the sun yet gave neither pain nor heat. From his golden aura the words that became the Book of the Way issued forth in great bands of pure thought. They soared out past the clouds and were fixed into the sky like stars, visible even in the light of day¡¦. " The Golden Dawn of Nirvana "


Prasad Mahasvati was a painter long ago whose most infamous work is pointed to as a cautionary example to all others who would stray down his path. He was a most talented young Bosalian, and on the 200th anniversary of Nirvana Chakravati's Acension, he had been asked by the Golden Dream Temple to paint a picture of Nirvana as he delivered his teachings. The painter was both excited and anxious, as trying to capture such a culturally famous image would be a most demanding task, and the ceremony would be in a month. With great trepidation he begun, having a stretched canvas of pure white Bodhisattva bark three by four meters in size. He read over and over the scene from many texts, looking for clues as to the colors that were seen and the best angle from which to show Nirvana. The first day he spent hours upon hours standing in his studio, staring at the expansive canvas, pondering. Eventually his life-partner Ghina who came and spoke sweet words of comfort, calming his troubled spirit and encouraging him. The two embraced and Prasad agreed that he would start work tomorrow, finding no sense in straining himself too much on the first day.

On the second day up to the fifth day, Prasad gathered the right ingredients and pastes to create the colors he would use in the painting. The canvas still remained untouched, but Prasad assured himself that he still had plenty of time and the choosing of the colors would be the most difficult part of the painting. On the night of the fifth day, his life-partner Ghina had to pull him away from his studio again, having spent the majority of the previous three days at his studio. Prasad apologized deeply for spending so much time away from her, but she understood how much this project meant to him and how much it would mean to the people, she easily forgave him. ' As long as you do not forget our anniversary three weeks hence, ' she playfully rebuked him. Prasad smiled and swore to her that that day he would leave the studio, no matter the state of the painting, to be with her.

From the sixth to the seventeenth days were filled with endless sketching, erasing and testing different hues. The large canvas remained untouched yet, Prasad practicing on smaller canvases until he felt he was ready for the real thing. The painter ate, slept and lived in his studio, constantly sending letters of apology scribbled on anything he could lay his hands on to Ghina. ' I will be with you on the 26th, my love. On that day I will make up for my long absence from you and toast to a hundred years together for our future. '

It was not long before the passage of time and lack of sleep had begun to take their toll on the young painter. His aura flickered the sickly blue-green of depression, as for all of his sketching still he could not find the right way to portray Nirvana. This painting would ensure his name for generations to come, millions of Bosalians viewing his work, evaluating his work each and every year. Even the smallest flaw would mar the perfect beauty of that divine moment, and such a thing was inexcusable. Such was the pressure the poor young artist was under. Worse still, days had passed without even having seen his beloved or heard her voice to cheer him, and the thought of making Ghina lonely and upset because of his self-imposed exile gnawed upon his concentration.

Every time Prasad advanced upon the canvas, brush in hand, something seemed wrong; maybe the color was too bright, maybe the angle should be adjusted, perhaps the light source should be shifted. Sleep eluded Prasad as much as inspiration did, and for hours on end he would pace back and forth in front of the blank canvas, his mind a clutter of feelings of failure, loneliness and sorrow. Upon seeing his haggard face in a pool of water one day Prasad refused to let Ghina see him, for fear that she would become even more distraught. She visited him daily, but all he allowed was for her to hear his voice. No amount of begging would get Prasad to leave, Ghina's worrisome pleas making Prasad's heart break with indecision.

On the twenty-third day a PsiWeaver from the Golden Dream Temple came calling upon the artist, to see the progress of the work. It took so long fro Prasad to answer the door that for a few moments that PsiWeaver was wondering if perhaps the artist was not there. Just as the acolyte was about to leave the door creaked open a crack. The PsiWeaver peeked in but the door slammed closed. ' I cannot let you see the work just yet, I fear. ' Prasad called through the door. ' Assure the High PsiWeaver that it will be completed before the ceremony ten days hence. '
The acolyte stood confused for a moment before replying:
' But you are mistaken, the ceremony is in seven days. I hope my visit does not make you think we do not have faith in your talent. I will tell the High PsiWeaver of what you have said and trouble you no long. Good day. '

With this, the acolyte left, leaving Prasad clutching his robes fouled with a week's worth go paint and sweat. He had been off by three days, and still he canvas sat blank. Fear gripped the young man's heart. All were relying on him, and he had accomplished nothing. What's more, it was three days before his anniversary, and right now even an evening not working on the project would be too much. Prasad gathered his jangled nerves as best he could and resolved to complete the painting with then next two days.

Ghina traveled from her and Prasad's home on the evening of the twenty-sixth. She was dressed in her finest gown and smelled of thrill blossoms, Prasad's favourite flower. She had no doubt in her mind that her life partner was on the verge of completing his task, and he would come to the studio doors exhausted, a little ragged, but beaming with pride at what he had done. Prasad had a great talent, and this work would become legendary. As she neared the studio she saw all the windows were covered and on the double doors a smudged canvas was hung. Ghina saw that the canvas had writing on it, written in haste with a paintbrush tinted with several colors. It read thus;

     Dearest Ghina,
     I have posted with note here because I cannot bear
     to have you see me in such a state. Inspiration has
     eluded me and my great work is nothing but a jumbled
     image in my head. I cannot do nothing but beg your
     forgiveness and hope that you can still love me for
     shutting you out for so long. It rends my heart to
     write this, but, my love, I cannot join you this eve.
     Curse my feeble mind and numb hands for their
     great sloth! Nothing I can say excuses my actions,
     yet I have sacrificed too much already to stop now.
     Please forgive me.
     Eternally yours,
     Prasad Mahasvati

Upon reading this tears welled up in Ghina's eyes and she looked up at the dark, silent building that housed his studio. ' In all the time I have known you, you have always kept your word. Now you keep me away from you and torture me with images of you suffering alone in the dark. I beg you one last time, leave this tomb you have made for yourself and come to me! '

Ghina waited in vain, for no answer was forthcoming. Inside, Prasad was huddled down on the floor, staring with weeping eyes at that mocking expanse of white canvas. He could not leave, he could not give up after so much suffering. He could not face her like this. He stayed like that until he was sure Ghina had left and shakily picked himself off of the floor. In his state of churning emotions that threatened his sanity, a revelation hit the young artist, causing him to stagger. The image that had eluded Prasad for so long came to him in a moment of crystal-clear revelation. He dove for his paints and started working feverishly on the canvas, for fear that it would leave his head as suddenly as it had arrived.

Days passed, and no one knew anything about how Prasad was faring. On the twenty-ninth day the same acolyte and a team of movers arrived at the studio to transport the painting to the temple and get it ready for the ceremony. They knocked loudly, and no one answered. Finally, they movers broke down to the door, no one within responding to them. Inside what was once a neat and brightly lit room was now in complete disorder, broken canvases, dried pools of spilled paints, and half-eaten meals a week old littered all surfaces. They found Prasad passed out from exhaustion at the foot of the painting, a paintbrush still clutched in his hand. The movers carried the unconscious artist from the room while the acolyte eased the young artist's suffering as much as he could with his powers. The painting was covered with a thick dark cloth, the last thing Prasad had managed to do before collapsing, and the acolyte peeked under for an instant before leaving to be sure that it was complete. Once Prasad was taken to a nearby wellness centre the painting was moved to the temple in anticipation of its unveiling.

Two days later found a huge crowd at the Golden Dream Temple, all waiting to see this masterpiece that a young artists had struggled so hard and endured so much to finish. Prasad was there as well, still weakened from his ordeal, but determined to see his duty through to the end. Supporting him was Ghina, who after seeing him in such a pitiful state, had spent every waking moment beside him. Their love was back, Ghina forgiving Prasad for his passion for his medium.

After a brief introductory song and speech the High Psiweaver grabbed a hold of the rope that would pull the sheet down in front of the painting and gave it a tug. The black material rippled down away from the painting, and a gasp went through the crowd. A split second of amazement suddenly turned to moans of despair.

The picture was just as every person in the room had imagined the appearance of Nirvana to be like. Such a mastery of color and depth had never been seen before. But after viewing the painting a psychic wave of sorrow and pain washed over the sensitive minds of the Bosalians, causing many to turn away and weep. This is what gives the painting its name. All the negative emotions that Prasad had felt had been absorbed into the special bark the canvas had been made of. Any person who viewed it experienced all the terrible emotions Prasad himself had felt. The artist broke down with grief at the sight of his painting giving so many people so much pain, a scar on his psyche that would haunt him until his dying day.

Prasad's Shame can still be viewed, tucked away in a shadowy corner of the temple's cellar, yet very few want to. His story is an example of the evils of putting too much focus on images and physical things, ironically, just what Nirvana had sermonized against. Perhaps Prasad's soul can find some solace in knowing that while his work will never be viewed in public again, his tale will teach others the folly of the material path better than any PsiWeaver ever could.
