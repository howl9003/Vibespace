Query: Organism
by Jason Balmer

The SS Scuttlebutt slowly crept toward the horizon, the spidery tethers stretched taut by inertia.
"Dangit, Horace! I though you told me you had that under control."
Horace briefly looked up from the Universal Display Console at the back of the Skipper's
head, carefully composing himself as he did. "I'm on it."
He leaned back in the old acceleration chair, one of the few fixtures to have survived the many years the Scuttlebutt had been in service, waiting for the new algorithm to take effect, his head cocked at an angle to keep a wary eye on the tethers. The UDC was almost 2 generations old.
The Scuttlebutt shivered as the tethers coiled back in, bringing it closer to the asteroid's surface. Horace watched them, the distant sunlight morning dew on a strand of silk. "Done, Skipper."
He forced his gaze back to the UDC, checking to ensure the inertial nullifier, scavenged like everything else on the old ship, was working within optimal range. He let out a short sigh of relief, carefully pitched to ensure the Skipper wouldn't overhear it.
"Kole, get me a display on the target, now," the Skipper barked. Horace looked back to the porthole, his eyes narrowing as he saw Kole's ghostly expression reflected from the darkened glassteel. Kole had reached his limit.
"Quit yer daydreaming and draw in number 4 tether, Horace."
Horace nodded to himself and entered the protocol. It was time.

The Scuttlebutt closed in on its target, silently, patiently. The vector imparted to the small asteroid by three days of careful maneuvering was barely enough to get them to the intercept point on time. That was how you planned these things. The Mechs were smart, in their way, but their sensors were no match for a mile of rock and human cunning. If the newly installed power management systems proved their worth.
"Time to start her up, boys!" the Skipper cackled, his voice naked with greed.
The Scuttlebutt shook itself to life as the anti-matter coils began charging. The bridge took on a dull red sheen, the combat light pulsing as power surged through the old ship's veins. Horace thumbed the console, switching back to manual controls, the holographic UDC dissolving into so many random particles. The Skipper's specially shielded UDC sprang to life, vomiting a globule of yellow and red light into the centre of the round bridge, at the tapered head of the old vessel which clung like a limpet to the rock.
Horace's head jerked back as the drive powered up. He cursed, stabbing at the controls to bring the inertial nullifier back online. "Re-routing ¡¦circuits burnt in start-up," he called over his shoulder, conscious of the Skipper's eyes boring into his head.
"Horace, Kaminski, Zambutu? On my mark. Three? Two ¡¦now!"
Horace killed the tethers, small charges severing the spools from the ship's belly. An instant later, the hull began to tremble as the enormous drive came online and the Scuttlebutt thundered toward its prey. Horace risked a brief glance out the portal, catching a glimpse through the distorting glassteel of the wallowing Enviritos cargo vessel as they banked. Stupid mechs. He looked back to the console, his eyes desperately searching for the green indicator that would tell him the inertial nullifier was working optimally. He let out his breath.
A telltale flash from outside the porthole caught his attention. The Mechs had finally noticed them and fired their drive, but it was already over.
They'd been too slow.
A chime rang out, barely audible over the dull groaning of the Scuttlebutt's aging frame, accompanied by a brief amber flash from the console. Horace re-focused his attention, scowling and bringing up the HUD, his fingers stabbing at the wavering holograms, desperately re-routing power relays. Over his shoulder, he could hear the Skipper barking orders, forcing the protesting Scuttlebutt to turn.
"Fire!"
The HUD turned a fiery orange as the charge leapt across the narrow gap between the two vessels. Horace knew what was happening over there: the massive EM pulse was frying circuitry. Lights were failing, navigation and steering were suddenly proving impossible, the pitiful weapons were turning silent in the strange claws the Mechs had for arms. As for the Mechs, themselves? The weaker ones would be losing consciousness, right about?
"Skip, those life signs are dropping like flies," Kole called out.
The Skipper laughed and tore his gaze away from the inert ship. Horace tried to avoid his eye, but couldn't. "Time we go rustle us up some Mechs, girls! Suit up!"

One of the things he hated about Mechs was their eyes, or sensor apparatus, or whatever you called it. They didn't use the same spectrum as living creatures. Another one of the things he hated about the Mechs was that they didn't breathe, and so they didn't have niceties such as life support systems on their vessels, forcing him into an EVA Suit full of stale sweat and the sour tang of fear, using a helmet spotlight to blindly grope his way around a strange ship.
"Hey, Hora, c'mere, check this one out."
Horace turned cumbersomely, his gloved hand pressing against a wall for support. He pushed off, fighting a wave of nausea, and floated toward Kole.
"What?"
Kole's suit radio hissed as he softly whistled. "I think we got a live one, here."
Horace forced himself to relax, bending slightly at the knees to cushion his impact with the wall. He managed to avoid bouncing off again, grabbing hold of a frame like structure - a console? a bed? - and turning to grin at Kole's laughter.
"Much better, compadre. Hey, the Mech's a small one, ya think it's like a Mech baby?"
Horace flipped himself around to face the ceiling, or wall, or whatever it was, and used his hands to walk toward to the inert figure. "Full beam," he sub vocalized, the EVA Suit esponding with a floodlight. "You go check it out, I got your back."
He could see Kole's helmet turn toward him, but his face was obscured by the helmet's polarization, a response to the spotlight.
"Yeah, sure? You just make sure you got my back, you hear me?"
Horace nodded, sending the spotlight panning up and down across the still and steely room. "Sorry."
"De nada, compadre." Horace watched Kole come to a perfect stop beside the Mech with a soft puff from the attitudinal thrusters on his harness, the vapour sparkling in the fierce spotlight. Kole bent and looked at the Mech, then prodded it with the butt of his rifle. "Hey, Hora, how do you tell if it's still ?it's still working?"
Horace fought off a shrug. "Use your scanner, they should emit some form of electrical activity when online."
Kole laughed softly. "Yeah, I forget. We got presets back on the 'butt."
Horace nodded slightly, the spotlight dipping as Kole swept the forearm mounted suit scanner over the knot of metallic limbs that was the Mech. He swallowed dryly and frowned, tucking his chin down inside his helmet to reach the small straw with the corner of his mouth. Anything to wash away the taste in his mouth.
"Holy ?hey, Hora, it's still alive, I think.
"Horace glanced at Kole from the corner of his eye, the spotlight briefly bobbing toward him."Yeah, man ?so what do we do? Skip says we only need the dead ones, right?
"Horace swallowed and wet his lips. "Well, yeah. It's some of the circuitry, the rest is only scrap. You may as well k?"
Kole pushed back in alarm, sending him floating toward the opposite surface of the cuboid room. "Hora, it moved, man, it moved!"
Horace felt something shift in his stomach. "Uh, Kole, just, just be cool, ok? Ok?" He looked over to the Mech, his mouth already dry again. From somewhere within the nest of tools or legs or whatever they were, Horace could see movement.
"Kole?"
Horace turned his head to look for his partner. "Kole? Is this some kind of joke, again? This isn't funny, Kole."
Horace saw a brief blur of movement and snapped his head back toward the Mech. He was distantly aware of himself reaching for something, but he knew not what.
A voice buzzed in his ear. "Query organism: designation?"
Horace's hand closed around something. He pulled himself toward it, his eyes fixed on the Mech in the corner, it's limbs slowly unfolding. "Query organism: designation biological, adjunct species: designation human, adjunct unit identification?"
He felt his entire body begin to shake. The Mech's voice ? signal ?
continued to run in his head, a harsh buzzing sound that set every single pore on his skin ablaze. Horace felt himself begin to detach from the situation. He was aware that he was panicking, and yet, that awareness grew ever more separate from himself, until he felt like an observer. Like he was watching a holo.
"Query organism: purpose?"
Horace felt himself slowly turn away from the Mech, hauling himself around, the spotlight languidly sweeping across the walls, or ceilings, or floors.
He could finally see Kole there, floating motionless in the middle of the rear or front of the room, a small plume of crystals flowering from what must have been a hole in the smartfibre of his EVA Suit. Distantly, he heard himself realize this meant Kole was probably dead. He continued turning, and as his peripheral vision sent the first signals to his brain, he felt whatever it was in his stomach drop even further, and then he knew he was screaming as he looked down at his hand and saw he was holding the arm or leg or tool of another Mech.
The buzzing in his ear stopped.
He did not dare to move.
A different buzzing come into his head. This one was less harsh, perhaps.
Somehow, he knew it was the signal of the Mech before him.
"Query organism: designation Horace. Follow?"
Horace was sure he must have nodded. He could dimly sense himself moving, through a hatch in the rear or front of the room, past the billowing shroud surrounding Kole, through long, clinically straight corridors and tubes.
Somewhere along the way he became aware that his spotlight had gone off, and lights had come on in the ship, yet the lights were insufficient to dispel the fog that had fallen across his senses. After some time he found himself entering a larger chamber, flooded with a pale red light, festooned with alcoves and dozens of the spidery Mech forms. Most were inert. He suddenly understood that this was a medlab.
"Query Horace: query purpose."
Horace floated above the scene. Of the perhaps 50 Mech forms in there, all but 4 were still. He turned back to the Mech. "I don't understand," he heard himself say.
"Query Horace: query purpose."
He could only gape stupidly, mesmerized.
"Query Horace: pause."
He watched the Mech numbly as one of it's limbs unfolded and described a gentle arc around the room, stopping when his eyeline intersected the limb and one of the inert Mechs. Suddenly he understood. "You mean why?"
The Mech whirred in his ear. "Unit status: destroyed; primary adjunct, secondary adjunct, tertiary adjunct: unit self. Query Horace: why?"
All he could do was weep.
